01 — Overview

A second tool, used deliberately.

The TL;DR — what the work was, why it mattered, and what it changed.

SurveyMonkey's CMS is excellent for scalable marketing content, but a handful of moments — major reports, trends pieces, brand moments — needed something more editorial and more interactive than the CMS was built to express. I led the evaluation and adoption of Framer as a complement to the CMS, then designed and built the first wave of high-impact landing pages on it: SurveyMonkey Trends and Workplace Trends for a New Era.

The work wasn't just visual. I partnered with engineering on a reverse-proxy approach that lets Framer-built pages live under the SurveyMonkey domain, defined the Amplitude event plan so these experiences could be measured rather than just launched, and helped the team articulate when to reach for Framer versus when to stay inside the CMS. The result is a repeatable model for shipping immersive marketing — brand-forward, interactive, measurable, and operationally honest.

02 — Context

A CMS built for scale, not for moments.

The CMS is the workhorse of SurveyMonkey's marketing site — hundreds of pages across regions and campaign cycles, all flowing through a shared component library. By 2024 it was doing exactly what we'd designed it to do: ship a high volume of high-craft pages quickly, with brand consistency and accessibility baked in.

But scale and craft aren't always the same brief. Major reports, category-defining trends content, and brand moments wanted to be slower, taller, more editorial — with scroll-driven storytelling, interactive data exploration, and visual layering the CMS components weren't designed to compose. Forcing those moments into the CMS either watered them down or stretched the system into shapes it would have to pay for later. The question wasn't "is the CMS broken" — it wasn't. The question was: where does the CMS end, and what should sit alongside it?

Background

SurveyMonkey's marketing surface served four audiences across four locales. The CMS scaled content well — but editorial, interactive brand moments needed a different surface entirely.

03 — Problem

Brand moments deserved a different gear.

Trends reports and anchor brand moments are different in kind from a product page. They're narrative, paced, and visually unusual on purpose — the page is the report, not a wrapper around a PDF download. The CMS was an excellent fit for everything around those moments but a poor fit for the moments themselves.

At the same time, a parallel tool couldn't be a free-for-all. If Framer pages drifted from the brand, opened in a different domain, broke analytics, or sat outside SEO, the cost would land back on marketing anyway. The real problem wasn't "which tool is best" — it was how to introduce a second tool without losing the operational discipline the CMS work had built.

Problem

Teams trying to ship editorial, interactive brand moments were forced to either water the experience down to fit the CMS or detach it from the rest of the site entirely.

What we heard

  • Brand described the CMS as "right tool, wrong altitude" for trends pieces.
  • Growth wanted to measure trends content like the rest of the site — microsites kept arriving uninstrumented.

What was missing

  • Engineering had been asked several times whether a non-CMS experience could live under the SurveyMonkey domain. The answer needed to be "yes, here's how."
  • Marketing leadership wanted clarity: when do we reach for Framer, and when do we stay in the CMS?
"We don't need a different brand for these moments. We need a different gear." — Brand stakeholder, scoping workshop
04 — My Role

Design lead, and the person on the keyboard.

It also meant translating between teams that didn't usually work together on web: partnering with engineering on the reverse-proxy infrastructure, with growth on the Amplitude plan, with brand and content on the story.

Plainly

I treated this as a platform problem in disguise. The deliverable looked like two beautiful landing pages — the actual deliverable was a repeatable model for shipping immersive web work.

05 — Strategy

Bridge craft and infrastructure.

Treat Framer as a deliberate second surface, not an escape hatch.

A second surface has a defined role, a defined relationship to the CMS, an infrastructure path, and a measurement story. An escape hatch is whatever someone reaches for when the main tool feels too small — and accumulates the same kind of debt the CMS did before we modernized it.

What that meant in practice was naming the things we wouldn't do. We wouldn't move CMS-shaped work into Framer just because it was new. We wouldn't ship Framer pages on a separate domain. And we wouldn't launch without analytics — if we couldn't measure the experience, we couldn't justify the model long-term.

Strategic bet

A deliberate second surface with a defined role, infrastructure path, and measurement story — not an escape hatch that accumulates debt.

07 — Key Decisions

The four forks that shaped the model.

  1. D · 01

    Adopt a second tool, instead of stretching the CMS

    Extending the CMS to cover editorial, scroll-driven storytelling would have meant adding edge-case components for a handful of pages a year — the exact pattern that fragmented the CMS the first time.

    Kept
  2. D · 02

    Reverse-proxy on AWS, not a separate subdomain

    Standalone microsites read as detached from the brand and split SEO authority. Routing Framer pages under surveymonkey.com via a proxy kept the domain story honest — one site to visitors, two surfaces to the team.

    Kept
  3. D · 03

    Analytics requirements ship with design, not after

    Microsites that arrive uninstrumented don't get a second pass. Defining the Amplitude plan during design forced clearer questions and made the experiences legible to growth from day one.

    Kept
  4. D · 04

    Write a decision model before the next request arrives

    Without a clear rule for Framer-versus-CMS, every project would have re-litigated the question. A short, shared decision model meant other teams could choose correctly without coming back to me each time.

    Kept
08 — System & UX Solution

A second surface, with guardrails.

Three pieces holding the model together: a decision framework, a storytelling pattern set, and an infrastructure + analytics layer.

The solution isn't a Framer page — it's a way of using Framer. Framer covers the editorial, narrative, interactive moments. The CMS covers everything around them. A reverse-proxy makes the two read as one site. An Amplitude event plan makes the work measurable. And a short decision model keeps teams from drifting either direction.

Figure 04 — Trends landing · scroll narrative + interactive moments
Fig 04A scroll-driven editorial story with interactive data sections, navigation, and a measurable arc end-to-end.
Decision model

Reach for Framer when the page is the story — editorial, paced, interactive. Stay in CMS when the page is one of many of its kind. If unsure, default to CMS — Framer earns the page; the CMS is the floor.

Storytelling patterns introduced

  • Scroll Chapter — full-height narrative beat; pacing primitive
  • Interactive Stat — hover, toggle, or scrub to explore data
  • Section Nav — persistent chapter index

Infrastructure patterns

  • Proxy routing — Framer pages live under surveymonkey.com
  • Ambient CTA — persistent, scroll-aware conversion surface
  • Exit Moment — designed end-of-story transition back to core site

09 — Collaboration

Designed alongside engineering, growth, and brand.

This project touched more functions than most web work does. It lived at the intersection of brand, growth, engineering, and content — and each had a legitimate claim on the outcome.

Key partners

  • Engineering — reverse-proxy architecture, caching, redirects, and SEO behavior on AWS
  • Growth — Amplitude event taxonomy, scroll-depth instrumentation, attribution
  • Brand & Content — voice, narrative arc, hierarchy, and pacing of trends content

How we worked

  • Weekly cross-functional check-ins during build
  • Analytics plan reviewed and approved before launch
  • Brand sign-off on interaction patterns, not just static mocks
  • Post-launch retro that fed the Framer playbook
"This is the first time a marketing page shipped with analytics that growth could actually read on day one." — Growth PM · cross-functional partner
10 — Impact

What changed downstream.

Qualitative outcomes from the first two Framer launches and the model they established.

Engagement

Immersive pages earned deeper reads.

Scroll depth and section-level engagement on Trends pages exceeded comparable CMS-built content, validating the editorial investment.

Measurement

Day-one analytics, not "we'll add it later."

Both launches shipped with a full Amplitude event plan. Growth could read section views, interaction events, and exit moments from the first day of traffic.

Repeatability

A model, not a one-off.

The Framer-vs-CMS decision model, storytelling patterns, and infrastructure setup are now used by the broader team without coming back to me each time.

The reverse-proxy approach means Framer pages live under surveymonkey.com — one site to visitors, search engines, and analytics. SEO authority stays consolidated; brand perception stays unified. Quantitative engagement metrics are tracked internally and available on request.

11 — What I Learned

Three things I'd do the same, two I'd change.

  1. L · 01

    Write the decision model before the second project asks for it.

    The playbook we wrote after the first launch prevented every subsequent project from re-litigating "Framer or CMS?" That clarity was the highest-leverage artifact in the whole initiative.

    Keep
  2. L · 02

    Ship analytics with the design, every time.

    Defining the Amplitude plan during design — not after launch — forced better design questions and made the experiences legible to growth immediately. Non-negotiable going forward.

    Keep
  3. L · 03

    Treat the infrastructure as part of the design brief.

    The reverse-proxy wasn't a technical afterthought — it was a design decision. "One domain" shaped everything from SEO to brand perception. Design leads should own that conversation early.

    Keep
  4. L · 04

    Start responsive design earlier in Framer.

    We designed desktop-first and adapted to mobile later than we should have. Framer's responsive tools are capable but require intentional setup from the start — retrofitting breakpoints is expensive.

    Change
  5. L · 05

    Document interaction patterns in code, not just mocks.

    The storytelling patterns (scroll chapter, interactive stat, ambient CTA) were documented as design examples. They should have shipped as Framer component snippets — the gap between "documented" and "reusable" was wider than expected.

    Change
