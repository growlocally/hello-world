01 — Overview

From pages to pathways.

A three-year thread of navigation, IA, and product-storytelling work — read separately as nav projects, read together as one piece of design about how a growing product gets explained.

A growing product ecosystem doesn't just need more pages — it needs clearer pathways through them. Over three years I led a thread of work focused on exactly that: a global navigation redesign, a chapter navigation system for long-form content, an information architecture strategy for MRX (Market Research) in the logged-out site, an exploration of how Programs should appear on the marketing surface, and a broader set of product-led growth experiences that help users educate themselves before they ever sign up.

Read together, they're one piece of work: helping users move from awareness to understanding to action, and helping cross-functional teams decide where new product stories should live without fragmenting the site. The thread that ties them is information architecture as product storytelling — the menu, the page hierarchy, and the order of explanation are part of the product experience, not packaging around it.

02 — Context

A bigger product, a fuller site.

By 2022 SurveyMonkey wasn't one product anymore. There was the flagship survey product, expanding solution areas for HR, CX, and market research, a developing MRX (Market Research Experience), and longer-running motions like Programs that didn't fit cleanly into any of the existing buckets. Each had its own audience, its own narrative, and a legitimate claim on the marketing site. The site, like most growing sites, had been built one campaign at a time — the navigation reflected where the company had been, not where it was going.

Logged-out visitors were doing more pre-product work than ever. They were learning about features, comparing solution areas, evaluating fit for their team, and trying to figure out what SurveyMonkey actually was before they signed up. The site was doing product education whether anyone had designed it for that job or not — and doing it unevenly. The work in this case is the work of making it do that job on purpose.

From pages to pathways

Logged-out marketing surfaces aren't a brochure stack. They're the product education layer — where visitors learn what SurveyMonkey offers, find their way to the area that matches their job, and self-orient before they sign up. Treating navigation, hierarchy, and IA as pathways through the product — not as menu chrome — is the difference between a site that grows with the company and one that fragments under it.

03 — Problem

A site users had to decode.

Two problems, one cause: visitors couldn't always find the path through the product, and teams couldn't always agree on where new product stories should live.

From the outside, the marketing site asked visitors to do a lot of decoding. The top-level nav had grown to reflect history, not strategy. Solution areas and product surfaces overlapped in unhelpful ways. Long-form content didn't give readers a way to orient inside a page or move between sections without scrolling. Visitors arriving from search ads or organic landed on a single page and often couldn't tell what else was nearby, what they should read next, or how the page they were on connected to the product.

From the inside, every new product story re-opened the same conversation: where does this live on the site? MRX needed to introduce a developing market-research experience without fragmenting the existing portfolio. Programs needed a way to surface a more complex concept that didn't fit the single-product page pattern. Each ask was reasonable on its own; together they were producing a navigation system that grew by accretion.

What was getting in the way — visitor side

  • Global nav reflected history, not strategy — items added over years with no clear hierarchy of solution vs product vs resource.
  • Long-form content shipped without consistent in-page wayfinding — readers couldn't see where they were in a 4,000-word guide.
  • PLG surfaces were shaped by whichever team owned them, not by a shared model of the acquisition journey.

What was getting in the way — internal side

  • MRX needed an introduction strategy — without one, each team proposed a different shape and the conversation kept restarting.
  • Programs didn't map to the existing page templates and needed a way to be explained the site hadn't been designed to express.
  • The safest path in every new conversation was always to add another item to the menu — and the menu was already overfull.
Problem statement

Visitors needed clearer paths through a growing product ecosystem, and internal teams needed clearer models for where new product stories should live — but neither was getting it, because navigation, IA, and long-form content wayfinding were being treated as page-level work instead of as the product education layer they actually were.

"The menu tells you what the company has built. It doesn't tell you what to do." — Internal workshop, navigation discovery
04 — My Role

UX lead across the thread.

I led the navigation, IA, and product storytelling work as one connected effort. That meant leading individual initiatives end-to-end where I owned them outright (chapter nav, MRX IA exploration, Programs framing), and acting as the systems-and-strategy partner on the bigger ones (global nav, PLG surfaces) where ownership sat with other teams. The connective work — translating between initiatives, keeping the framing consistent across them, and making sure no two of them quietly contradicted each other — was as much the role as the individual designs.

What I led
Chapter navigation primitive · MRX logged-out nav strategy · Programs exploration · cross-cutting IA framing
What I partnered closely on
Global nav redesign · PLG web surfaces · solution-area positioning
What I shipped personally
Option-shaping matrices, IA audit maps, chapter-nav spec, Programs experiment frame, the “pathways over pages” framing artifact
What I didn't own
Brand identity, the implementation of global nav, growth experimentation infrastructure
Plainly

I treated these initiatives as one body of work, not a parallel stack. The deliverable for any given one looked like a nav redesign or a content navigation system; the deliverable across them was a more legible site, and a clearer way for the org to decide where new product stories should live.

05 — Strategy

Marketing UX is product UX.

If a visitor's first hour with the company is on logged-out pages, the logged-out site is part of the product. Design it that way.

The bet on one line: treat navigation, IA, and product storytelling as the product education layer — not as packaging. Once you accept that frame, the choices change. The menu isn't a list of what the company has built; it's the entry to a set of pathways into the product. Long-form content isn't a piece of marketing copy; it's a workspace the reader has to be able to navigate. MRX isn't a new tab; it's a story that has to fit into the visitor's existing model of SurveyMonkey without overwriting it. Programs isn't a page; it's an experiment in how a complex concept gets introduced.

The corollary was that creating options was a deliverable. Most of the hard decisions in this thread were cross-functional — brand, growth, product marketing, content, engineering, and leadership all had legitimate inputs. My job wasn't always to land the single right answer; it was often to lay out the option set, name the tradeoffs, and make the decision smaller and faster for the people who had to sign off. Options work is design work.

Strategic bet

Design pathways, not pages. The unit of design is the journey from awareness to understanding to action — pages are stops on a path, nav is the path's shape, and optimizing pages without designing the path between them is the trap.

06 — Process

Audit, frame, option, decide, ship.

Five phases I ran across each initiative — overlapping in time, not sequenced. The thread held even when the projects didn't.

  1. P · 01

    Audit

    Walked the site as a visitor would. Mapped the existing IA, the points of friction, and the spots where new product stories kept colliding with old structure.

    Phase
  2. P · 02

    Frame

    Named the shared framing — pathways over pages, marketing UX as product UX — and tested whether stakeholders could repeat it back. The frame had to land before the options would carry weight.

    Phase
  3. P · 03

    Option

    For each initiative, laid out two-to-four real options with tradeoffs. Not a recommended path — the option set, scored against the same criteria, so the room could choose.

    Phase
  4. P · 04

    Decide

    Walked options through cross-functional reviews. Translated between brand, growth, product, content, and engineering vocabularies. Closed decisions in writing.

    Phase
  5. P · 05

    Ship

    Designed the chosen path end-to-end — desktop, mobile, accessibility, fallback states — and partnered with engineering and content on rollout, including experiment framing where the answer wasn't yet known.

    Phase

07 — Key Decisions

Five forks across the thread.

Each was a decision I either led directly or shaped heavily — and each held across multiple initiatives, which is what made the thread feel like one piece of work.

  1. D · 01

    Treat nav and IA as the product education layer

    Logged-out visitors do most of their product education before sign-up. Once you accept that the marketing site is doing that job, navigation stops being chrome and becomes part of how users learn the product. That reframing is what made the rest of the thread coherent.

    Kept
  2. D · 02

    Ship options, not opinions, on cross-functional calls

    A strong opinion arrives as a wall to negotiate. A clear option set arrives as a starting point. Option-shaping moved decisions faster than advocacy did, and the room kept its agency.

    Kept
  3. D · 03

    Pick the MRX nav pattern by what visitors think they're buying

    Product switcher, hub, sub-routes, house-of-brands — each was defensible. The deciding question wasn't internal structure; it was whether visitors understood MRX as part of SurveyMonkey or as a sibling brand.

    Kept
  4. D · 04

    Frame Programs as an experiment, not a page

    Programs was new enough that nobody knew the right shape for it yet. Treating it as a final page would have forced a guess; treating it as a hypothesis to test let us learn the shape from real behavior.

    Kept
  5. D · 05

    Standardize chapter navigation as a content primitive

    Long-form content was shipping with bespoke nav on every guide. Building chapter nav as a reusable primitive — desktop sticky, mobile sheet, accessible by default — meant every long-form page got readable wayfinding without paying for it again.

    Kept
08 — System & UX Solution

Four initiatives, one thread.

Each project is described as its own piece — but the underlying frame is shared: nav and IA as the product education layer, with options laid out for cross-functional decisions and experiment framing where the shape was still unknown.

Figure 03 — Awareness → understanding → action · the pathway view
Fig 03The pathway frame the thread sits inside — global nav at awareness, chapter nav at understanding, MRX and Programs at the new-product seam, PLG surfaces at the handoff into the product.

I. SurveyMonkey navigation redesign

The global nav had grown by accretion — top-level items added over years, hierarchies blurred between product, solution, and resource, mobile behavior brittle, accessibility uneven. The redesign clarified hierarchy, rebalanced what got top-level placement, and rebuilt the dropdown, sticky, and mobile behaviors as a single connected system. I sat on this initiative as IA and option partner rather than sole owner; the contribution was helping the room agree on what the nav was for before agreeing on what it should look like.

Before

Top-level items reflected company history. Solution and product surfaces overlapped without a clear hierarchy. Dropdowns were inconsistent across sections. Mobile collapsed everything into a single overstuffed sheet. Accessibility on focus, keyboard paths, and ARIA was uneven.

After

A hierarchy organized around what visitors are trying to do — product, solutions, resources, pricing — with consistent dropdown behavior, deliberate sticky behavior, a usable mobile pattern, and accessibility baked into the components. The menu became a wayfinding tool, not a sitemap.

II. Chapter navigation for long-form content

Long-form pages — guides, reports, deep solution pages — were shipping with bespoke nav per project. Readers couldn't see where they were in a 4,000-word page, couldn't jump between sections, and lost orientation on mobile. I led the design of a chapter nav primitive: sticky on desktop, surfaced as a usable sheet on mobile, with progress orientation, keyboard support, and screen-reader-friendly section labeling. Once it was a primitive, every new long-form page inherited it.

Fig 04The chapter nav primitive — sticky index on desktop, mobile sheet with progress, accessible labels. Built once, reused on every long-form page.

III. MRX logged-out navigation strategy

MRX needed a way to live within the existing site without fragmenting it. I led the IA exploration: four real options, scored against the same criteria, with the tradeoffs named for leadership. The decision wasn't mine to make — but the option set is what let the decision get made.

  1. A

    Product switcher · top-level toggle

    Pros: reads MRX as a co-equal product. Strong if MRX is positioned as its own line. Cons: doubles the surface visitors have to orient inside; risks splitting brand authority.

    Option
  2. B

    MRX hub · dedicated section under SM

    Pros: a defined home for MRX storytelling without breaking global nav. Easiest to ship. Cons: can feel like a sub-area rather than a peer if not framed carefully.

    Option
  3. C

    Sub-routes in product nav

    Pros: minimal nav cost, MRX inherits site authority. Cons: easy to miss; harder for MRX to develop its own narrative voice over time.

    Option
  4. D

    House of brands · sibling brand

    Pros: maximum independence for MRX brand and IA. Cons: high cost to launch and maintain; risks fragmenting visitor's mental model of SurveyMonkey.

    Option

The option set was the artifact. Each was scored against the same criteria — visitor mental model, launch cost, narrative latitude for MRX, long-term IA risk — and presented to leadership side-by-side, not stack-ranked. The conversation moved from "what should we do?" to "which of these tradeoffs are we choosing?" — which is the conversation a decision can actually close in.

IV. Programs exploration

Programs was a more complex concept than the existing solution-page template could express. I led the exploration of how it should appear on the marketing site — not as a final page, but as a set of options framed as experiments. Where should it live? How should the concept be introduced? Where does animation help and where does it overwhelm? The framing turned an ambiguous brief into a forward motion: we could test versions, learn the shape from behavior, and ship the version that earned it.

Experiment frame

Treat the Programs surface as a hypothesis. Define what visitors should understand after reading. Ship two-to-three structurally distinct versions. Read the results as evidence about the concept, not just the page.

Storytelling primitives

Use motion and progressive disclosure to introduce a complex concept gradually. Anchor the explanation in a concrete use case, then expand outward. Reserve denser detail for readers who scroll past the orientation.

V. Product-led growth surfaces

PLG surfaces are where logged-out turns into logged-in — sign-up entry points, template-to-product hand-offs, the seams between the marketing site and the product itself. I partnered with growth on those surfaces as part of the same thread: each one a moment where a visitor's pathway either continued cleanly into the product or quietly stalled. Treating those moments as part of the same pathway logic as the nav redesign — rather than as growth's separate territory — kept the journey continuous instead of segmented.

Principle

The nav doesn't have to do everything. It has to make the next click obvious.

09 — Collaboration

A thread held by many hands.

Navigation, IA, and product storytelling sit where brand, product marketing, growth, content, and engineering all have legitimate authority. The job was making sure they were arguing about the same thing.

None of these initiatives lived inside one team. Brand had a say in how nav looked. Product marketing had a say in how products were positioned. Growth had a say in what the surfaces should produce. Content had a say in voice, length, and structure. Engineering had a say in what was buildable and maintainable. My role across the thread was to keep all of those views in the same conversation — not by smoothing them, but by translating them into a shared vocabulary and a shared option set.

How we worked

  • Recurring cross-functional working sessions
  • The same framing — pathways over pages — opening each one
  • Option-shaping matrices before any decision review
  • Shared vocabulary across brand, growth, PMM, content, eng
  • Decisions closed in writing and pinned to the artifact

How we made decisions

  • Two-to-four real options, scored against the same criteria
  • Tradeoffs named, not stack-ranked
  • "Which mental model are we backing?" framing for IA calls
  • Experiment framing for genuinely new surfaces
  • Reversible decisions: ship, learn, revisit in 4–6 weeks

10 — Impact

Better decisions, better paths.

Qualitative impact. The thread didn't produce a single launch you can screenshot — it produced clearer decisions, clearer paths, and a shared frame the org could keep using.

For visitors

A site that wayfinds.

Visitors arriving from search, ads, or campaigns can orient faster — top-level nav reflects what they're trying to do, long-form pages give them progress and chapter access, and the surfaces feel like a connected site instead of a stack of campaigns.

For cross-functional decisions

Options, not arguments.

Option-shaping artifacts changed how the room decided. Brand, growth, product marketing, and engineering could argue tradeoffs against the same criteria — instead of restarting the framing every time.

For new product stories

Less ambiguity about where things live.

MRX and Programs gave the org chances to practice the "where does this live?" question. Both ended up with cleaner, more deliberate placements — and the patterns from those decisions have carried into later product launches.

For long-form content

Pages you can navigate.

Chapter nav became a reusable primitive. Long-form content stopped shipping with bespoke wayfinding; readers got consistent orientation, progress, and accessibility across every guide.

For PLG

Marketing UX, treated as product UX.

The seams where logged-out turns into logged-in were treated as part of the same pathway as the nav and IA work — not as a different team's territory. The journey became continuous instead of segmented.

For the team

A frame the room could repeat.

"Pathways over pages" became something stakeholders could repeat back unprompted. A shared frame is what lets a thread of initiatives feel like one piece of work — and what makes the work survive the next reorg.

The honest summary: this work didn't produce a single hero launch. It produced a more navigable site, a better way to decide where new product stories should go, a reusable primitive for long-form content, and a framing the org now uses unprompted. Connective work compounds — when it's done well, you don't see it; you just notice the friction that didn't happen.

11 — What I Learned

Five notes I kept.

Lessons written for the next thread of navigation, IA, and product-storytelling work.

  1. L · 01

    Marketing UX is product UX.

    The most useful reframing was simple: the logged-out site is part of the product. Visitors learn the product there before they ever sign up. Once that's accepted, nav, IA, and long-form wayfinding stop being "marketing concerns" and start being design surfaces with the same craft bar as anything inside the app.

    Keep
  2. L · 02

    Options move faster than opinions.

    Strong opinions are useful on individual projects; on cross-functional IA work they become walls. A clear option set — same criteria, tradeoffs named — moves the room faster than the strongest advocate could. The deliverable on a hard decision is often the matrix, not the answer.

    Keep
  3. L · 03

    Pick the question, not the answer.

    For MRX, the breakthrough was naming the right question — "do visitors think of this as part of SurveyMonkey or as a sibling brand?" Once the question was the right one, the decision shrank from "which of four patterns?" to "which mental model are we backing?" Reframing the question is the highest-leverage move in IA work.

    Keep
  4. L · 04

    For novel surfaces, frame as experiments.

    Programs taught me that for genuinely new product concepts, the right artifact is a hypothesis, not a final page. Experiment framing converts ambiguity into forward motion and lets behavior — not opinion — settle the shape. It also lets the room ship without committing more than it needs to.

    Keep
  5. L · 05

    Make connective work visible, or lose it.

    A thread of related initiatives can read as a stack of small projects unless someone writes the connective story explicitly. The frame, the artifact, the option set, the pattern: writing each of them down — and using them in the next project — is what turns the thread into a piece of work.

    Keep
