01 — Overview

A patchwork, recast as a platform.

The TL;DR — what the work was, why it mattered, and what it changed.

SurveyMonkey's marketing site is the company's front door, but the CMS behind it had grown into a patchwork of one-off page templates, drifting components, and accumulated brand debt — fast at first, expensive to maintain at scale. The bet was to recast it as a modular platform built on a small vocabulary of layout primitives, a token system, and a pattern library the whole marketing org could ship through.

I led the design side of the modernization — owning the system's shape, partnering with engineering on the implementation, and working across Brand, Content, and Growth to make the new vocabulary stick. The deliverable wasn't a screen; it was a system designed to make the next thousand pages easier to build than the last hundred were.

02 — Context

A marketing site with more history than system.

Where the CMS was, what it was being asked to do, and which constraints were real versus inherited.

SurveyMonkey's marketing site has been the workhorse of acquisition for more than a decade — hundreds of pages, multiple regions, frequent campaign cycles, and several historical brand eras layered on top of one another. The CMS had been added to year over year, mostly by cloning whichever template was closest to the next idea. It worked. It just didn't scale.

This case is about the marketing CMS specifically — the system Marketing, Brand, Content, and Growth use to build and ship pages. Adjacent surfaces (the product UI, the app's design system) had their own teams and their own systems. The CMS had to share a brand language with them without sharing their architecture, and it had to keep the live site running through every step of the migration.

03 — Problem

Marketing wanted to move at the speed of ideas.

The CMS made them move at the speed of engineering tickets — and brand drift, accessibility debt, and experimentation cost all followed from there.

The simplest way to describe the old CMS: every page was a snowflake. New pages cloned the nearest old page and edited from there. Every campaign cycle accumulated a new variant of a hero, a feature row, a footer CTA. Brand updates required hunting down each instance by hand. Accessibility was inconsistent and unowned. And because every variant was a custom build, growth experiments were expensive enough that the team ran far fewer than they wanted to.

Problem statement

Teams trying to ship campaigns on a weekly cadence were forced to file engineering tickets or clone-and-customize legacy templates because the CMS had no shared vocabulary of components, tokens, or layout primitives.

What we heard

  • Marketing PMs described the CMS as "build it once, maintain it forever." A page was easier to launch than to edit a month later.
  • Growth couldn't run more than a handful of experiments per quarter — each variant required a custom build.
  • Brand caught drift only after pages were live. Updates were retroactive and expensive.
  • Engineering was triaging marketing tickets that should never have needed an engineer.

The knock-on effects

  • Publishing velocity slowed to the cadence of the eng ticket queue.
  • Brand expression was uneven across the company's most-visited surface.
  • Accessibility was inconsistent and unowned across hundreds of pages.
  • The cost of variation crowded out the experiment itself.
"Every page in the CMS is a snowflake. We don't have a system — we have a graveyard of templates." — Marketing Engineer, kickoff workshop
04 — My Role

Design lead on the system, not the screens.

UX Designer / Manager. On this initiative, design lead and systems strategist.

I owned the system's shape — what it was, what it owned, what it would and wouldn't support — and worked across teams to make that shape stick. That meant designing the primitives, defining the tokens, drafting the governance model, and spending a lot of time in working sessions translating between Brand, Engineering, Content, and Growth.

The deliverable was a system, not a screen. The job was to make the next thousand pages easier to build than the last hundred were — and to make the case, repeatedly, that the CMS deserved to be designed like a product.

05 — Strategy

Treat the CMS like a product, not a tool.

Invest in primitives that compose into pages, not templates that try to predict pages.

The strategy fit on one slide: stop adding templates, start designing primitives. Templates encode the page in front of you; primitives encode the pages you haven't designed yet. We chose primitives, and built every other decision — tokens, governance, rollout sequencing — around that single commitment.

What that meant in practice was naming what we wouldn't do. We wouldn't add a new page template every time marketing had a new need — that path was the problem, not the solution. We wouldn't rewrite the editor into a monolithic visual builder, tempting and expensive as that was. And we wouldn't lock the system down: governance over gatekeeping.

Primitives over templates.
A small set of layout primitives that compose into anything is more durable than a large set of templates that compose into nothing. The vocabulary is the system.
Tokens are the contract.
Type, spacing, color, and container widths live in tokens. If a brand decision can't be expressed in tokens, it probably shouldn't ship — and a brand refresh shouldn't require a rebuild.
Speed and consistency are the same output.
Brand consistency and publishing velocity aren't opposites. They're both signals that the system is doing its job — holding the floor so teams can move faster on top of it.
Governance, not gatekeeping.
Anyone can use the system. New patterns get reviewed before they enter it. The system stays small as it grows, and the team trusts itself to extend it without re-fragmenting it.
06 — Process

From audit to vocabulary.

Twelve months in five phases — most of it spent reducing what looked like complexity into a smaller, more honest set of shapes.

Phase 01
Audit
Inventoried every live marketing page; cataloged the components, layouts, and one-offs in the wild.
Phase 02
Frame
Mapped 60+ unique "layouts" to a much smaller set of underlying shapes. Identified the primitives that, composed, accounted for the majority of real pages.
Phase 03
Define
Drafted the primitive API, the token model, the pattern-library taxonomy, and the governance model. Whiteboards before Figma.
Phase 04
Pilot
Rebuilt a high-traffic product page and a campaign landing page entirely from the new primitives. Used those two pages as the proof of concept.
Phase 05
Roll out
New pages on the new system from day one; legacy migration in waves. Governance group stood up alongside.

07 — Key Decisions

The four forks that shaped the system.

Each one had a reasonable counter-argument. Each one held up under the next year of use.

  1. D · 01

    Layout primitives, not page templates.

    A Section / Row / Stack / Column / Grid vocabulary composes into anything marketing can imagine; a Hero / Features / Pricing / FAQ taxonomy fights you every time marketing imagines something new. The template approach was what produced the patchwork in the first place.

    Kept
  2. D · 02

    Tokens at the core, not hand-coded values.

    Spacing, type, container widths, and responsive breakpoints all live as tokens. The brand system becomes the contract — not a PDF, not a Figma file, but a layer the CMS itself consumes. A brand refresh now lands by changing tokens, not by re-touching every component.

    Kept
  3. D · 03

    Governance, not gatekeeping.

    Marketing's velocity was the whole point; a review queue would have re-introduced the bottleneck we were trying to remove. Anyone can use the system, anyone can propose a new pattern, and a small standing group reviews proposals weekly. Trust over toll booths.

    Kept
  4. D · 04

    Designed for experimentation from day one.

    Growth needed to spin up variants without an engineering ticket. Building variant-friendly composition into the primitives — rather than bolting an A/B layer on top later — was a deliberate early call. It cost more up front and paid back the moment growth started running tests at the cadence they wanted.

    Kept
08 — System & UX Solution

A small vocabulary, composed without limit.

Seven layout primitives, a token system connecting type / spacing / container / responsive behavior, and a pattern library built on top of both.

The system is built around a deliberately small vocabulary. Marketing composes pages by nesting primitives — Sections wrap Stacks wrap Grids wrap Columns — with every spacing, type, and color decision flowing from tokens. Patterns (Hero, Feature Grid, CTA Block, Testimonial Row) are higher-level compositions of primitives, never bespoke components. New patterns are encouraged; new primitives are rare events.

Before · After

Before
To ship a campaign landing page, marketing filed a ticket. Engineering cloned the nearest template. Design reviewed for brand drift after the fact. The page shipped two-to-three sprints later — slightly inconsistent, with accessibility addressed unevenly.
After
Marketing composes the page in the CMS from primitives. Brand, spacing, type, and accessibility floor are inherited from tokens. Pages ship on the same day they're scoped, with brand expression coming out of the system instead of being enforced on top of it.

09 — Collaboration

Who was in the room.

Design doesn't happen alone. Credit the people, and name the working pattern.

The working setup was lean by design: weekly working session with PM and engineering lead, open Figma file with comments-as-conversation, critique on Thursdays. Shipped behind a flag, watched live for two weeks before opening up. Most alignment happened asynchronously — a well-written Loom saved a lot of meetings.

A
Marketing PM
Product Management

Owned campaign delivery timelines and was the clearest voice for publishing velocity. Our weekly sync was where scope decisions got made and held.

B
Engineering Lead
Front-End Engineering

Built the primitive API alongside the design definition. Pairing on the component authoring model early saved significant rework — design and implementation were never more than a sprint apart.

C
Brand Lead
Brand & Visual Identity

Token values were a shared decision. The brand-to-token mapping was the most important alignment artifact the two teams produced together — it made a brand refresh a token update, not a rebuild.

D
Growth Lead
Experimentation & Growth

Made the case internally for variant-friendly primitives. Their requirements shaped the composition model more than any other team's — and they were the first to see the payback when the experiments started running.

Working pattern

10 — Impact

What the work moved.

Qualitative first. If numbers are available and honest, they go in the supporting paragraph — not the headline.

For the user

Pages ship on the day they're scoped.

Authors compose in the CMS from primitives. Brand, spacing, type, and accessibility floor are inherited. Engineering review is a check, not a bottleneck.

For the team

A vocabulary, not a graveyard.

The patterns introduced became shared shorthand across product, engineering, and marketing — speeding subsequent work on adjacent surfaces and reducing design review cycles.

For the business

Growth experiments run on cadence.

Variant-friendly primitives unlocked the experiment volume Growth had wanted but couldn't afford. The cost of variation dropped; the number of tests per quarter went up.

Brand has stopped flagging cross-page drift in weekly QA. Engineering escalations to design dropped measurably. Locale variants share a visual rhythm without explicit coordination. Quantitative results are tracked per pilot campaign — I'm comfortable speaking to those numbers in a live conversation.

"The system felt designed with us instead of handed to us. That was the difference." — PMM, partner team
11 — What I Learned

The notes I kept.

Four lessons, written as practical advice to my next self. Not platitudes.

  1. L · 01

    Make the authoring experience your hero.

    The component design was the easy half. The 10× gain came from designing the CMS form a marketer fills in — that's where velocity actually lives.

    Keep
  2. L · 02

    The earliest sketch was right about the structure, wrong about the tone.

    Trust the bones of an early direction even when the surface treatment is bad. The structural insight rarely changes; the visual layer almost always does.

    Keep
  3. L · 03

    Governance is a design problem.

    Who can add a component? Who can override a token? Documented and answered up front, those questions save quarters of friction later. Underdocumented governance re-introduces the snowflake problem under a different name.

    Keep
  4. L · 04

    The hardest part wasn't the design — it was holding the frame.

    The team had three reasonable directions to choose from. The job was less to invent a fourth and more to defend the chosen one through six weeks of build. Primitive-over-template is a principle that needs repeating at every sprint review.

    Keep
