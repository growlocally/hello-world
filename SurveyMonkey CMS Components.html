01 — Overview

The building blocks, modernized.

A multi-year thread of component work on the marketing CMS — read separately as UI updates, read together as one body of systems work on the library every page depends on.

Marketing sites move at the speed of their components. Over three years I worked on the building blocks behind SurveyMonkey's marketing site — columns, quote cards, logo walls, vertical tabs, SEO tables, announcement banners, calculators — turning a set of rigid, accreted components into flexible primitives that designers and content creators could compose without filing a request for every new shape.

The thread connecting them is a frame I held throughout: components are the operational infrastructure of a marketing site. Get them right and the team's velocity, consistency, and quality all rise together.

02 — Context

Components as operational infrastructure.

SurveyMonkey's marketing site runs on a CMS-backed component library — columns, quotes, logo walls, tabs, banners, calculators, and the dozens of smaller pieces those compose into. Content creators pick components, fill them with content, and ship pages. Designers contribute new pages by composing existing components first and reaching for engineering tickets second. The library is the operational infrastructure: every page that ships passes through it.

By 2022 the library had grown by accretion. Components had been added project by project, often shaped for the page in front of the team at the time. Many had become rigid — limited widths, fixed image positions, prescriptive content slots — so that what looked like "a small new layout idea" usually required a new component instead of a new arrangement. The library had quietly stopped being a system and become a catalog.

From catalog to system

The brief wasn't "refresh the components." It was to turn the building blocks into flexible, composable primitives — so the team could solve new page needs by combining existing components instead of designing new ones. That reframing is what made every project below feel like one body of work.

03 — Problem

A catalog, not a system.

Individual components weren't ugly — they were inflexible. Every new page need produced a new component, and the library grew without getting more useful.

The visible symptoms were what you'd expect: a one-column module that didn't quite hold the width a section needed, a logo wall that stacked awkwardly on mobile, a quote card that worked for one kind of testimonial and not the next, a tab component that felt heavy on phones, an announcement banner that fought with cookie banners and other site chrome. None of these were emergencies. Each one was the kind of small drag that, accumulated across dozens of pages, made every new page slower and less consistent than it had to be.

The underlying problem was structural. The library had been built with components that tried to anticipate specific pages instead of providing flexible primitives. When a new page need showed up that the existing components couldn't quite express, the path of least resistance was to add a new component. Over time that created a catalog: lots of options, narrow flexibility, easy to file in but hard to compose from.

Where the library was rigid

  • Layout components had rigid widths, content slots, and image placements — small layout ideas needed new components instead of new arrangements.
  • Quote components couldn't flex across the range of social proof patterns the marketing team actually needed.
  • Logo walls broke down on phones — fixed grids meant logos got too small or too few.

Where the library was wrong-shaped

  • Vertical tabs worked on desktop but felt heavy on mobile; microinteractions were missing.
  • SEO surfaces (tables, calculators) were high-traffic but treated as content rather than tools — usability fought search.
  • The announcement banner sat at the bottom, fought with cookie banners, and underperformed iteration after iteration.
Problem statement

The CMS needed stronger, more flexible building blocks — not just visual refreshes. Without them, every new page need produced a new component request, the library grew without getting more useful, and the team paid a steady tax in inconsistency, slow publishing, and one-off design fixes.

"We don't need a new component — we need the one we have to bend a little more." — Content creator, intake conversation
04 — My Role

Component strategist, often hands-on.

The role split in two directions. On the systems side, I held the frame across the whole thread — pushing each component project to be a chance to add flexibility, not to add another item to the catalog. On the hands-on side, I designed many of the individual components: redesigning the one-column module's flexibility, building out the quote ecosystem, leading the logo wall and carousel work, the vertical tab redesign, the SEO table and calculator updates, and the announcement banner replacement.

What I led
The flexibility frame across the thread · column components · the social proof ecosystem (Quote Card, Marquee Quote) · logo walls + scrolling carousel · vertical tab redesign · SEO table & calculator · announcement banner replacement
What I partnered on
Engineering on component APIs and the configurable-vs-forkable boundary · content on authoring slots, defaults, fallbacks · brand on type scale and rhythm · growth & SEO on high-traffic surfaces · accessibility on focus, labels, keyboard
What I shipped personally
Component audits, system maps, ecosystem groupings, the dual front-end + authoring designs, responsive behavior specs, accessibility contracts
What I didn't own
The underlying CMS platform, brand visual identity, growth experimentation infrastructure
Plainly

The deliverable was the library, not any single component. The components in §8 are the parts I designed or led; the value is in what they let the team do next.

05 — Strategy

Flexibility, not more components.

Every project was a chance to add composability to the library — not a chance to add another entry to the catalog.

The bet on one line: solve new page needs by making existing components more flexible, not by adding new components. A library grows by getting bigger; a system grows by getting better at composing. The same instinct shows up in every project below — when a new page need arrived, the first move was to ask whether an existing component could bend to it, and only if the answer was a clean no would a new component get proposed.

Treating components as operational infrastructure also meant designing for two audiences at once. End users see the front-end experience — layout, polish, responsive behavior, accessibility. Content creators see the authoring experience — slot structure, defaults, fallback states, and how predictable the component is once it's filled with real content. Most of the unforced errors in CMS work come from optimizing only one of those audiences.

Strategic bet

Make existing components bend, not add new ones. Flexibility is cheaper than catalog growth — only fork into a new component when the new use case is structurally different, not just stylistically.

  1. S · 01

    Make existing components bend, not add new ones.

    Flexibility is cheaper than catalog growth. Every new page need is a candidate to extend an existing component's range — only fork when the use case is structurally different.

    Principle
  2. S · 02

    Design for the author, too.

    A component is a UX artifact for two users — the visitor and the author. Slot structure, defaults, fallback behavior, and the predictability of the result count as design.

    Principle
  3. S · 03

    Group components into ecosystems.

    Quotes aren't a single component — they're a social proof ecosystem. Logo walls and carousels are a credibility pattern. Designing the ecosystem prevents drift between pieces.

    Principle
  4. S · 04

    Responsive is part of the component.

    Mobile behavior isn't a downstream concern — it's a constraint that shapes the design. Components that don't have a mobile shape don't exist yet.

    Principle
  5. S · 05

    Accessible by design.

    Focus, labels, keyboard paths, and screen-reader behavior are part of the component, not a retrofit — especially on interactive primitives like tabs, carousels, and banners.

    Principle
  6. S · 06

    Brand, SEO, and CMS are co-equal.

    A marketing component lives at the intersection of brand expression, search performance, and CMS authoring. Treat all three as primary; single-axis optimizations don't ship clean.

    Principle
06 — Process

Same five phases, component by component.

A repeatable shape I used across every component project — so the work compounded into a library instead of into a stack of disconnected redesigns.

Figure 01 — Component audit · library map with overlap and one-offs flagged
Fig 01The audit view. Library laid out by ecosystem, with overlapping components and one-off forks flagged — the map that decided where flex-extension was the right move.
  1. P · 01

    Audit

    Looked at where the component was being used, what it was being asked to do, where it broke down, and which page needs had spawned related one-offs. The audit usually named the right scope.

    Phase
  2. P · 02

    Frame

    Decided whether the project was a flex-extension (stretch the existing component), a redesign (rebuild the same role), or a replacement (the old pattern wasn't worth iterating). Most projects were flex-extensions.

    Phase
  3. P · 03

    Design

    Designed the front-end and the authoring experience together — slot structure, responsive behavior, accessibility, and visual polish in one pass. Mobile was a primary surface, not a follow-on.

    Phase
  4. P · 04

    Implement

    Paired with engineering on the API, the props, and the responsive logic. Closed contracts in writing so the component's flexibility was real, not theoretical.

    Phase
  5. P · 05

    Adopt

    Walked the component through content and design partners. Documented the use cases, the defaults, and the boundaries. A component without adoption is a fork waiting to happen.

    Phase

Fig 02The dual surface every component project produced — visitor-facing layout, CMS authoring interface, and responsive states designed in one pass.
07 — Key Decisions

Five system decisions that held across projects.

Each had a reasonable opposite. Each one kept the work from becoming a stack of disconnected component redesigns.

  1. D · 01

    Default to flex-extension. Only add new components when forced.

    Catalog growth is the trap a marketing CMS falls into one project at a time. Defaulting to flex-extension on each request kept the library converging toward a system instead of diverging into a catalog.

    Kept
  2. D · 02

    Design the authoring experience as design work.

    If a component is beautiful on the front-end but punishing in the CMS, content creators stop reaching for it — and design quietly starts filing one-off requests on their behalf. Slot structure, defaults, and fallback states are design surfaces.

    Kept
  3. D · 03

    Group related components into ecosystems.

    A single quote card couldn't carry every social-proof shape. Designing Quote Card and Marquee Quote together — as a social proof ecosystem — clarified what each piece was for and stopped sibling drift. Same move for logos and columns.

    Kept
  4. D · 04

    Replace, don't iterate, when the pattern is wrong.

    The bottom-anchored announcement banner was being iterated on every quarter and underperforming through each iteration. The right move wasn't a better version; it was a different pattern.

    Kept
  5. D · 05

    Hold brand, SEO, and CMS as co-equal constraints.

    SEO tables and the calculator lived at the intersection of brand expression, search performance, and CMS authoring. Designing for one would have undercut the other two — triangulation made the components better than any single-axis optimization would have produced.

    Kept
08 — System & UX Solution

Six themes, one library.

Grouping the work by theme — not by component name — is how it reads as a system instead of a list. Each theme is a place where the library got more flexible, more consistent, or more usable in a way that compounds.

Figure 03 — Themed library map · layout · social proof · interactive · SEO · banners · authoring
Fig 03The library viewed by theme. The components below cluster into six groups; each cluster is a place where the system became more useful.

Theme I · Layout flexibility

The columns are the most-used components in the library — almost every marketing page composes from them. Making them more flexible was the highest-leverage move in the whole thread.

One-Column
Added flexibility around width, content alignment, and image placement. Modernized long-form page sections and improved visual rhythm without forking into specialized components.
Two-Column
Improved ratio options, responsive stacking, and content-slot flexibility — supporting more adaptable storytelling without needing a new component for every layout idea.
Three-Column
Reworked density, reflow behavior, and content alignment so three-column sections held together at mobile widths instead of dissolving into a stack.
System decision · Layout

Columns are primitives, not templates. Each gained more configurability — widths, ratios, slot defaults, alignment — so designers and content creators could compose a wider range of pages from the same three components. The number of layout components went down; the layouts the team can ship went up.

Theme II · Social proof ecosystem

Social proof wasn't one component — it was a family. Designing Quote Card and Marquee Quote together let the ecosystem cover the full range, with each piece having a clear job.

Quote Card
A flexible quote pattern that works as a hero testimonial, a sidebar pull, or part of a grid. Attribution, avatar, and brand mark slots flex per use case.
Marquee Quote
A bigger, more editorial moment — for the testimonials that should anchor a page, not just decorate it. Designed to be the page's voice, not just a quote on it.
Logo Wall
Optimized for mobile presentation and logo density. The grid finally held shape at smaller widths, and the visual weight of the wall matched the intent of the page.
Logo Carousel
Added a scrolling logo carousel for more dynamic credibility moments — accessible, pause-on-hover, reduced motion respected. Where the wall is structure, the carousel is motion.
System decision · Social proof

Designed as an ecosystem, not as four separate components. The team can now reach for the right shape of social proof for the page — a featured quote, an oversized editorial moment, a static wall of logos, or a scrolling carousel — without having to invent a new pattern each time.

Theme III · Interactive content

Interactive components are where authoring, accessibility, and mobile behavior all collide. Getting them right pays back across every page that uses them.

Vertical Tabs
Redesigned for large content sets, with clearer microinteractions, a better mobile pattern, and keyboard-accessible tab behavior built in.
SEO Calculator
SEO landing page calculator redesigned to feel like a tool, not a content block. Inputs simplified, results made scannable, CTA paths clarified — and SEO needs held alongside, not against, usability.
Figure 04 — Vertical tabs · desktop & mobile · microinteractions and active state
Fig 04The redesigned vertical tab pattern. Microinteractions clarified the active state; mobile got a usable shape instead of a scaled-down desktop one.

Theme IV · SEO and utility patterns

High-traffic SEO surfaces are some of the most-visited pages on the site. Treating them as products instead of content blocks made them more useful — and didn't cost search performance.

SEO Table
Optimized for readability and scannability at scale. Dense tabular content stayed accessible to search while becoming easier to read, sort, and act on for real visitors.
SEO Calculator
Redesigned as an interactive tool rather than an SEO content shell. Visitors actually use it; SEO still gets the surrounding context it needs.

Theme V · Banners & announcements

Banners are easy to underestimate — they live around the page, not in it. But the wrong banner pattern fights with everything else on the site.

The announcement banner replaced an underperforming bottom-banner pattern with a more visible, brand-aligned design that cohabits cleanly with cookie banners and other site chrome. Built to be dismissed without losing its job.

System decision · Banners

Iterating on a pattern that's fighting the rest of the site is throwing good design after bad. The announcement banner got replaced, not refreshed — a different shape that did the job and didn't pick a fight with cookie banners or other chrome.

Theme VI · Authoring & responsive behavior

Two cross-cutting themes that touched every component above — and the part of the library that's hardest to see in screenshots.

Authoring efficiency

Slot structure, defaults, and fallback states designed so content creators land on a good result quickly. Components that look like options on a tray instead of forms to fill out. Predictability over configurability where the two compete.

Responsive behavior

Every component designed with mobile as a primary surface. Logo walls hold density; tabs become a usable mobile pattern; quotes stay legible; tables remain scannable on small screens.

Accessibility

Focus, labels, keyboard paths, and screen-reader behavior baked into interactive primitives — tabs, calculators, banners, carousels. Designed in during component work, not bolted on after.

Brand · SEO · CMS triangulation

Every component had to pass all three checks: brand expression, search performance, CMS authoring. Single-axis optimization isn't a library; it's a forking ground.

09 — Collaboration

A library held by many hands.

A component library only stays a library if every team that touches it sees its work in the result. The role was as much weaving as it was making.

Design owned the components' front-end shape and the authoring experience. Engineering owned the implementation, the prop API, and the maintenance cost. Content owned how components were authored in real life — what defaults made sense, what slots were used, what fallback states were needed. Brand owned visual treatment, type scale, and the rhythm components inherited from. Growth and SEO owned the high-traffic surfaces where component decisions had measurable consequences. Accessibility partners owned the bar; my job was to design to it from the start.

Engineering
Owned the implementation surface — prop API, responsive logic, maintenance. We paired on contracts so each component's flexibility was real in code, not just in mocks.
Content
Owned authoring and the voice that fills the slots. We co-designed defaults, fallback states, and slot structure so components landed on a good result quickly.
Brand
Co-owned type scale, spacing, and visual treatment across components. The library inherits brand instead of fighting it; brand stays expressed at the component level.
Growth & SEO
Owned search performance on SEO tables and calculator pages. We co-designed components that improved usability without undercutting search — neither side getting a veto.
Accessibility
Owned the accessibility bar — focus order, labels, keyboard support, screen reader behavior. Reviewed each interactive primitive before launch.
Design partners
The other designers using the library in their work. Their feedback during use — what bent, what didn't, what got forked — was the most reliable signal on whether the library was getting better or just bigger.
"Component projects ran in waves with a small cross-functional pod each time — same five phases, same partners, same checkpoints. The next component cost less than the last." — Working pattern across the thread
10 — Impact

Components that compose.

Qualitative impact. The deliverable was the library, and the library got more useful — for designers, content creators, engineering, and the visitors who never see it directly.

For designers

More flexibility, fewer one-offs.

New page needs are increasingly answered by composing existing components instead of designing new ones. The library bends; the catalog stops growing.

For content creators

Faster publishing, better results.

Authoring is more predictable. Slot structure, defaults, and fallback states let authors land on a good result quickly — less time fighting the CMS, more time on the work.

For consistency

Pages that belong to one site.

Components inherit brand, accessibility, and responsive behavior by default. Pages across the site feel like neighbors, not a stack of campaign decisions.

For mobile users

Components that work on phones.

Logo walls hold density. Vertical tabs become a usable mobile pattern. SEO tables stay scannable. Mobile is a first-class context across the library, not a follow-on.

For accessibility

Inclusive by default.

Interactive primitives ship with focus, labels, keyboard paths, and screen-reader behavior built in. The library's accessibility floor came up; nothing has to be retrofitted page by page.

For the system itself

From catalog to system.

The library is smaller in spirit, larger in range. Fewer components covering more page shapes. The relationship between design system principles and CMS implementation got tighter — design system and CMS pulling together.

The honest summary: this work didn't produce a hero launch. It produced a marketing site whose pages feel more modern, whose authors move faster, whose components bend instead of breaking — and a library the team can keep using without paying the catalog tax. Component work compounds; the next page is cheaper than the last because the components paid the cost up front.

"The same three columns now do what fifteen used to. We just designed them like they meant it." — My note, library retro
11 — What I Learned

Five notes I kept.

Lessons written for the next component library — my own, or someone else's.

  1. L · 01

    Flex-extension is the default move.

    Every new component request was a chance to make an existing component bend instead. Defaulting to flex-extension — and only forking when the new use case was structurally different — was the single most important habit. It's also the hardest one to keep, because adding a new component always feels faster in the moment.

    Keep
  2. L · 02

    The authoring experience is a design surface.

    Components that punish content creators get quietly abandoned. Designing slot structure, defaults, and fallback states with the same care as the front-end — and counting an authoring improvement as a real improvement — kept the library actually used.

    Keep
  3. L · 03

    Components are an ecosystem before they're a list.

    Social proof was a family before it was four components. Layout was a family before it was three columns. Designing the ecosystem clarifies each piece's job and stops siblings from drifting into overlapping versions of each other.

    Keep
  4. L · 04

    Know when to replace, not iterate.

    The bottom-anchored announcement banner taught me this one. Some patterns are wrong, and iterating on them throws good design after bad. Recognizing that — and arguing for replacement instead of refinement — is part of the systems job.

    Keep
  5. L · 05

    Mobile-first is a system position, not a project tactic.

    When mobile is a primary surface across the whole library — not a per-component negotiation — design conversations get faster and component results get better. Components that don't have a clean mobile shape just aren't done.

    Keep
