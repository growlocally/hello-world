01 — Overview

Leadership through clarity, not volume.

A multi-year effort to shape Web UX into a team with a clear remit, a written operating model, and a real seat at the table for marketing, product, and growth.

Web UX at SurveyMonkey operates across marketing, product, growth, content, engineering, research, and brand. The team's work focuses on the interactive layer between logged-out marketing experiences and product-led growth — the seam where the company's story meets its product. This case study is less about a single launch and more about the practice itself: how the team's remit was defined, how the operating model was built, how junior designers were mentored into it, and how Web UX came to be read as a strategic partner rather than a production function. Specific initiatives like CMS modernization and the Framer experiences live in their own case studies; this one is about the surface that made them possible.

02 — Context

A web surface owned by everyone.

The marketing site is the seam between the company's story and its product. Marketing owns the narrative and the funnel. Product owns the experience after sign-up. Growth runs experiments across the boundary. Brand owns the visual language across both. Content owns the words. Engineering owns the platform. Research owns the evidence. Every one of those teams has a legitimate claim on parts of the web surface — which is why, without a clear practice, web work tended to live in whichever team was loudest in any given quarter.

By the time I stepped into the management seat, the inputs were strong but the seam was thin. The team had talented designers and committed partners on every side; what it didn't have was a written remit, a shared operating model, or a clear story about what Web UX as a discipline actually did beyond shipping individual pages. The work in this case study is the work of building that surface.

Background

Web UX sat between seven partner disciplines. Each had a legitimate claim on the marketing site. None of them, alone, defined the team — and the team didn't yet define itself.

03 — Problem

A team without a written remit.

The organization needed clearer ownership around web UX work. Marketing, product, brand, content, and engineering all had overlapping needs, but the team itself needed a clearer remit, a stronger operating model, and better ways to show impact. Without that surface, every project re-negotiated scope, every junior designer absorbed conflicting signals about what good looked like, and the team's contribution got read as production volume rather than the connective work that actually held the surface together.

What I heard internally

  • "What does good look like here?" — in nearly every 1:1.
  • "I don't know whose call this is — design's, brand's, or growth's."
  • "Reviews keep turning into preference debates."
  • "Stakeholders learn each designer, not the team."

What partners were asking

  • Marketing wanted a single way to brief Web UX.
  • Engineering wanted PRDs and tickets to arrive with the design.
  • Growth wanted to know when to partner versus route elsewhere.
  • Leadership wanted a story they could tell about the team's work.
04 — My Role

Manager, mentor & still on the keyboard.

I held a deliberate split: roughly half management and practice-building, half hands-on design and systems contribution. The split was the point — managing without making would have left the team's craft unmoored from its leadership; making without managing would have left the practice unbuilt. I managed and mentored junior designers, helped define the team's responsibilities, project workflows, documentation practices, and design-system governance, and continued to contribute on systems, CMS modernization, and growth-led web work.

What I led
Web UX remit · operating model · review & intake practice · documentation conventions · mentorship
What I shipped personally
Team remit doc · PRD & intake templates · design review format · systems & CMS governance contributions
What I delegated
Project-level design ownership for junior designers (with framing & feedback support)
What I didn't own
Brand visual identity · product UX · marketing copy · platform engineering
05 — Strategy

Web UX as connective tissue.

Not a production function. Not a brand outpost. The team between the teams — the layer that makes marketing, product, growth, and brand cohere on the web.

The strategy had three commitments. First: frame Web UX as connective tissue, not a slice of marketing or product. Connective tissue is a real role — it's how marketing's story stays continuous with product's experience, how brand's language survives campaign cycles, how growth's experiments don't break the site. Naming that role gave the team a remit bigger than "the people who ship marketing pages" and smaller than "everyone who touches the web."

Second: practice as a deliverable. PRDs, Jira workflows, design review narratives, documentation, mentorship rituals — these are the artifacts that make a discipline legible. If the team only produced screens, it would keep getting read as production capacity. Third: manage and make. Stay close enough to the craft to mentor with specifics; stay far enough back to let designers carry their own work.

Strategic bet

A team with a written remit and a small, deliberate set of practices would do more for velocity, clarity, and quality than any single launch ever could.

06 — Process

From ambient to articulated.

Figure 01 — Practice-building arc · listen → frame → operationalize → apply → mature
Fig 01Five phases. Some sequential, most parallel — the practice and the projects had to be built at the same time.

The first phase was almost entirely listening: 1:1s with every designer and partner, mapping overlapping asks, conflicting expectations, and the moments the team's contribution was hard to read. The audit, not the answer, came first. From there I wrote the Web UX remit, the operating model, and the partner-relationship map — then tested the framing in partner reviews until stakeholders could repeat it back unprompted.

Operationalizing meant building intake, PRD templates, Jira workflows, design review formats, and documentation conventions — making "how we work" something you could read, not something you had to overhear. From there the practice earned its keep on live projects (CMS modernization, Framer experiences, navigation, growth surfaces), not in slideware. The final phase was maturation: mentoring designers into more ownership, codifying what worked, retiring what didn't, and rewriting the documents as the team grew.

07 — Key Decisions

Five forks that shaped the practice.

  1. D · 01

    Lead with the remit, not the roadmap

    The pull was to demonstrate value through volume — ship more, faster, louder. The longer-leverage move was to write what Web UX is for first, then let projects flow from that. Teams without a remit are judged by quarterly outputs forever.

    Kept
  2. D · 02

    Keep managing while continuing to make

    Staying close to the craft — on CMS modernization, Framer, systems work — meant feedback to junior designers carried specifics, conversations with engineering stayed credible, and the leadership stayed grounded in what shipping actually feels like.

    Kept
  3. D · 03

    Treat WDS and the marketing CMS as one system

    Design-system governance and CMS implementation tend to drift into separate conversations. Holding them as one — same tokens, same components, same intent — was the only way to stop the two surfaces from quietly diverging.

    Kept
  4. D · 04

    Mentor through ownership, not approval

    Junior designers learn faster when they own the call than when they wait for it. Feedback became about reducing ambiguity and naming the rubric, then stepping back — so the work showed the designer leading it, not the manager validating it.

    Kept
  5. D · 05

    Make the practice visible to executives

    Initially treated storytelling as a by-product. Revised to treat exec-ready framing as part of the work itself — connective contributions hide easily, and unwritten work is unfunded work.

    Revised
08 — System & UX Solution

A team you can read.

Four pieces holding the practice together: a written remit, an operating model, a documentation & review rhythm, and a mentorship loop.

Figure 03 — The connective-tissue view · Web UX in the seam between disciplines
Fig 03The frame the rest of the practice hangs from. Marketing on one side, product on the other; Web UX as the seam where brand, content, growth, engineering, and research meet.

At the base sits the remit: a short written description of what Web UX is for, what it owns, and where it hands off. Above that, the operating model — intake, framing, review cadence, hand-off conventions, and partner contracts. Above that, the documentation & review rhythm — PRDs, Jira workflows, narrative-led reviews, and exec-ready storytelling. At the top sits the mentorship loop: frame, feedback, ownership, repeat.

Principle

If the team only ships screens, it's read as production capacity. If it ships screens and practice, it's read as a strategic partner.

Figure 04 — Project framing examples · intake brief, review narrative, partner contract
Fig 04The three artifacts that turned vague requests into named problems, named partners, and named success criteria — before design started.

Leadership principles

  • Define the seam, not the silo.
  • Practice is a design deliverable.
  • Manage and make.
  • Systems before screens.
  • Mentor toward ownership, not approval.

Project types the team supports

  • Surface & structure — navigation, IA, conversion paths.
  • Systems & components — CMS modernization, WDS alignment.
  • Editorial & campaigns — Trends, Framer experiences.
  • Growth & experimentation — analytics partnership, test surfaces.
  • Product-led seams — onboarding entry, signed-in/out continuity.
09 — Collaboration

Seven partners, one seam.

Web UX only works through other teams. Most of the leadership job was translating between priorities — and making sure the translations were faithful in both directions. Marketing owned story and funnel. Product owned the logged-in experience. Brand owned the visual language. Content owned the words. Engineering owned the platform. Growth owned the experiments. Research owned the evidence. The working pattern wasn't one team room; it was a set of overlapping rooms, each with a named owner on the Web UX side and a short written description of what we owed each other.

How we worked

  • Named partner owners on the Web UX side
  • Weekly or bi-weekly cadence per partner
  • Short written contracts — what we owe each other
  • PRD-shaped intake before design starts
  • Narrative-led design reviews, not screen tours

How we made decisions

  • Frame the problem before debating solutions
  • Reversible calls: ship and revisit in cycle
  • One-way doors: write a memo and defend it
  • Brand & engineering vetoes documented and dated
  • Junior designers carry their own decisions
"The work was great. We just couldn't always tell anyone what kind of work it was." — Design partner, mid-tenure retro
10 — Impact

A team that's legible.

Qualitative impact. The point of this work was the practice itself; what's reported below is what the practice changed about how the team is seen and how it operates.

Ownership

Clearer Web UX remit.

What the team is for — and what it isn't — became something partners could state in a sentence. Projects stopped re-litigating scope at the start of every brief.

Mentorship

Designers with structure.

Clearer rubrics, more specific feedback, and explicit ownership boundaries let junior designers carry more of their work end-to-end — and tell whether they were doing it well.

Framing

Briefs that mean something.

PRDs, Jira workflows, and design review formats turned vague requests into named problems, named partners, and named success criteria — before design started.

Consistency

CMS and system in sync.

Treating WDS and the marketing CMS as one system reduced drift between product and marketing. Growth work and editorial work began to share the same primitives, not compete for them.

Positioning

Strategic partner, not production.

Web UX began to be invited into earlier conversations — strategy, brand, growth bets — instead of being briefed after the decision had already been made.

For me

Manager who still ships.

I stayed close enough to the craft to mentor with specifics — and far enough back to let designers carry the work. The hardest part of the role; also the one I most wanted to get right.

The honest summary: this work didn't produce a single launch you can screenshot. It produced a team more of the org can work with, practices that survive when the next manager arrives, and designers further along in their careers than they would have been without the structure. That's the kind of impact I most want to be evaluated on.

11 — What I Learned

Five notes I'd do the same & one I'd change.

  1. L · 01

    The team is the surface.

    I stepped into the manager seat thinking the work would be projects with a team attached. It's the other way around. The team is the surface; projects are how the surface gets used.

    Keep
  2. L · 02

    Staying on the keyboard wasn't optional

    Hands on the craft kept my feedback specific, my engineering conversations credible, and my decisions grounded in what shipping actually feels like. The risk wasn't bandwidth; it was credibility — and the credibility paid for the bandwidth.

    Keep
  3. L · 03

    Mentor for ownership, not approval

    The fastest way to slow a junior designer down is to be the gate they have to pass through. The slower, better way is to name the rubric, give specific feedback, and step back. Designers grow into the room you leave for them.

    Keep
  4. L · 04

    Write things down before you think you need to

    The remit, the operating model, the partner contracts, the principles — all of it felt obvious when I started. Writing it down turned it into something the team could carry without me. The unwritten version is the version that doesn't outlast my tenure.

    Keep
  5. L · 05

    Connective work needs storytelling

    Web UX's contribution lives in coherence, re-use, and friction that didn't happen — none of which screenshot well. Writing the story of the work isn't extra. It's how the connective work stays visible enough to be funded, defended, and grown.

    Keep
  6. L · 06

    I waited too long to write the remit

    I spent the first stretch demonstrating value through projects, assuming the remit would emerge from the work. It didn't. Next time, the written remit comes in week one — not month six.

    Change
