01 — Overview

Motion as a system, not a finish.

What the work was, what it produced, and the shift in how the team thought about animation.

SurveyMonkey wanted to explore how animation could make the marketing website feel more modern, engaging, and expressive. The work I led wasn't a motion library and it wasn't a sizzle reel — it was a practical strategy for where animation belongs, why it earns its place, and how to ship it inside a CMS-driven site without paying for it later.

I framed motion in two registers — storytelling moments and UX microinteractions — partnered with engineering on a Lottie vs. GSAP evaluation, and pushed the team toward research that could compare animated and static experiences honestly. The deliverable was a shared decision framework and a set of guidelines that designers and developers could use without re-litigating the question every time.

02 — Context

A site getting more expressive, and a team without a map.

Where the marketing web was, what motion was being asked to do for it, and the constraints that quietly shaped every decision.

By 2024, the SurveyMonkey marketing site was working hard. Hundreds of CMS-driven pages across regions and campaigns, a maturing component library, and a growing appetite — from brand, marketing, and product — for moments that felt less like a SaaS site and more like a publication. Animation was the natural next lever.

But the moment we said "let's use motion," the surface area exploded. Where? On every page, or a few? Built how — and by whom? What happens to performance on a mid-range Android in São Paulo, to a screen reader, to a CMS author trying to drop a hero on a regional landing page? Without a frame, animation could become decoration with a cost — inconsistent, inaccessible, expensive to maintain, and not obviously better than the static version it replaced.

03 — Problem

Motion the team wanted, without criteria to defend it.

A problem of decision-making more than a problem of craft.

Everyone agreed animation could elevate the site. Nobody agreed on when it should appear, who should build it, what good looked like, or how we'd know it worked. Designers brought references, engineers brought concerns, marketing brought campaign deadlines, and the conversation reset every time. The risk wasn't motion being bad — it was motion being arbitrary, and accruing cost across pages that would have to live with it for years.

Problem statement

The team saw value in animation, but had no shared system for when to use it, how to build it, or how to evaluate whether it improved the experience. Without that frame, motion drifted toward decoration — inconsistent, costly, inaccessible, and unmeasured.

What we kept hearing

  • From design — "We want it to feel more alive, but I don't know what the bar is."
  • From engineering — "Lottie or GSAP? Both? What does the CMS even let us drop in?"
  • From brand & marketing — "Competitors feel modern because they animate. Can we just… add motion?"

Quieter signals

  • Reduced-motion preferences ignored on hero animations.
  • Hero animations adding seconds to LCP on slower devices.
  • The same loader rebuilt three different ways across the site.
  • No shared easing scale — every component had its own.
"The question isn't whether to use animation. It's what animation is doing for the user — and what it's costing them when we get it wrong." — Working note, motion strategy kickoff

05 — Strategy

Motion as web UX, not visual preference.

The goal wasn't more animation. The goal was better storytelling, clearer feedback, and more memorable product communication.

The strategic move was to stop debating animations one at a time and start debating criteria. Once we had criteria, individual decisions became a lot less expensive. The bet, in one sentence: animation earns its place when it supports comprehension, feedback, or brand storytelling — and is built so that performance, accessibility, and the CMS author all stay whole. What that ruled out: motion as decoration, motion as competitive mimicry, and motion that couldn't be authored inside the CMS without bespoke engineering each time.

Motion must do a job.
Every animation has to point at one of four jobs — comprehension, feedback, affordance, or brand storytelling. If a reviewer can't name the job, the motion comes out.
Quiet by default, expressive where it counts.
Microinteractions stay calm and consistent. Storytelling moments are allowed to be ambitious — but only on the few pages where storytelling is the point.
Accessible first, animated second.
Reduced-motion is a real user, not a checkbox. Every animation must have a graceful static state, respect prefers-reduced-motion, and never carry information motion alone.
Performance is a design decision.
Weight, paint cost, and LCP impact are part of the design review — not a post-hoc engineering cleanup. If a motion can't ship inside its budget, it gets simpler, not louder.
Built to be shipped, not just shown.
Every motion pattern names its implementation path — CSS, Lottie, or GSAP — and how it lives inside the CMS. Prototypes that can't be reproduced don't count.
06 — Process

From moodboards to maintainable.

Collecting examples, sorting them by job, putting tooling on trial, and writing the parts that had to outlive any single page.

07 — Key Decisions

Where the strategy could have gone otherwise.

Four forks where the choice changed the shape of the system.

  1. D · 01

    Two categories of motion, not a single global library.

    Storytelling moments and UX microinteractions have different jobs, different audiences, and different cost profiles. Treating them as one bucket would have produced either over-restrictive guidance for brand work or under-restrictive guidance for everyday components. Splitting them let each category have its own bar.

    Kept
  2. D · 02

    Both Lottie and GSAP — chosen by use case, not by preference.

    The temptation was to pick one tool to standardize on. But the evaluation showed they're good at different jobs: Lottie for designer-authored, self-contained motion that ships through the CMS; GSAP for scroll-driven, runtime-responsive choreography on flagship pages. The framework names which tool fits which pattern.

    Kept
  3. D · 03

    Research-first, not launch-first.

    The team's instinct was to ship animated versions and watch the metrics. I pushed for designing a comparison — animated vs. static — across engagement, comprehension, and brand perception, so we could learn rather than assume. Slower up front, but it prevents motion becoming a thing we never question.

    Kept
  4. D · 04

    A decision framework, not a motion library.

    A library of approved animations would have aged badly — needs change, brand evolves, components get rewritten. A decision framework — "when to use animation, when not to, and how to choose how" — outlives any specific asset and forces every new piece of motion to defend itself on first principles.

    Kept
08 — System & UX Solution

A small system, holding a big idea.

Two motion categories, a tool decision, a research model, and the question every animation has to answer before it ships.

The system is small on purpose. Five principles, two categories, two tools sorted by job, one research framework, and one decision tree. Any designer or developer can hold the whole thing in their head — and any reviewer can use it to evaluate a piece of motion in under a minute.

Tool evaluation · Lottie and GSAP

Engineering and I scored both tools against the constraints that actually matter on a marketing site. Neither tool won outright — and that turned out to be the answer.

Lottie
Asset-based · JSON
Best for Designer-authored, fixed-shape motion that ships through the CMS — loops, icons, brand loaders, illustrative moments.
Workflow
Designer-led in After Effects, exported as JSON. Minimal developer handoff for standard loops.
Performance
Light when authored well; heavy if AE files aren't optimized. Demands export discipline.
Flexibility
Limited at runtime. Hard to react to scroll, viewport, or user input beyond simple triggers.
CMS fit
Excellent. JSON drops into a CMS field, plays in a component, no per-page engineering.
Maintenance
Asset-shaped. Updates mean re-exporting; risk of stale loops if owners change.
GSAP
Code-based · Runtime
Best for Scroll-driven, runtime-responsive choreography on flagship pages — product stories, interactive reveals, scenes that respond to the viewer.
Workflow
Developer-led, with motion direction from design. Slower to iterate, but precise.
Performance
Tunable. Strong on transforms; depends on engineering discipline for paint cost.
Flexibility
Very high. Scroll, input, timeline control, complex sequencing, conditional motion.
CMS fit
Limited. Belongs in bespoke page templates, not in author-facing CMS fields.
Maintenance
Code-shaped. Lives in the codebase; survives owner turnover better than assets.
The decision

Use Lottie where motion is content and needs to live inside CMS components. Use GSAP where motion is choreography on bespoke pages. Use CSS for everyday state transitions — never load a library to animate a hover.

When to use animation · decision framework

Before adding motion to a screen, run it through five questions. If any answer is no, the default is the static version.

01
Does this animation do a job?

Name the job in one phrase: comprehension, feedback, affordance, or brand storytelling. If you can only describe it as "delight" or "make it feel modern," it doesn't pass.

Yes →Continue to Q2.
NoShip the static version. Revisit when the job becomes clear.
02
Does it work better than a thoughtful static version?

Compare honestly. If a well-designed static state communicates the same thing, motion is adding cost without value. Force the comparison; don't assume.

Yes →Continue to Q3.
NoInvest in the static version. Cheaper, more accessible, often clearer.
03
Can it ship inside its performance budget?

Estimate weight, paint cost, and LCP impact on baseline devices. If the animation requires a budget exception, it gets simpler — not louder.

Yes →Continue to Q4.
NoSimplify the motion or replace with a static state. Performance is a design decision.
04
Is there a graceful reduced-motion path?

Every animation needs a static or instant counterpart for users who request reduced motion. Content can never live in motion alone. If you can't answer this, you don't have a finished design.

Yes →Continue to Q5.
NoDesign the reduced-motion state before continuing. It's part of the work, not an afterthought.
05
Is the implementation path named?

CSS, Lottie, or GSAP. Where it lives — CMS component, bespoke template, or shared library. Who maintains it. A prototype without an implementation path is a moodboard, not a deliverable.

Yes →Ship it.
NoPair with engineering before continuing. Motion that can't be reproduced doesn't count.

Research model · animated vs. static

So the system could keep learning, I framed how we'd compare animated and static versions of the same moment. The point wasn't to validate motion — it was to build a habit of asking whether it actually helped.

Looking at
Engagement (time on page, scroll depth), comprehension (recall and message clarity), brand perception (modern, trustworthy, distinctive).
Method
Paired prototypes — animated vs. thoughtful static — tested with comparable audiences across desktop and mobile. Qualitative interviews alongside the quant signals.
Posture
Designed to learn rather than to confirm. Motion was allowed to lose; that's how the framework earns its credibility.

09 — Collaboration

Held together by working partnerships.

A motion strategy is a cross-functional artifact. These are the people who kept it honest.

Motion strategy can't be written from a design seat alone. Engineering brought reality into the tool evaluation; brand brought the editorial ambition that kept the framework from going purely defensive; research kept us asking whether motion actually helped; the design system team made sure the guidelines could live alongside everything else.

How we worked

  • Weekly motion working group with design, engineering, and brand
  • Shared Figma library for principles and patterns
  • Decisions tracked in a single doc — next debate started where the last one ended
  • Open critique every two weeks with cross-functional reviewers

Who was in the room

  • Web engineering — performance traces and CMS integration tests
  • Brand & motion design — storytelling craft and editorial ambition
  • UX research — animated vs. static comparison model
  • Design system & CMS — component and authoring alignment
Working pattern

Weekly motion working group with design, engineering, and brand. Shared Figma library for principles and patterns. Decisions tracked in a single doc so the next debate could start where the last one ended — not at the beginning.

10 — Impact

A shared language, not a stack of assets.

The framework's job was to change how the team thought — and that's where the evidence lives.

For the team

A shared language for motion.

Design and engineering stopped having the same debate every sprint. Words like "job," "register," "budget," and "fallback" showed up in critiques — and shortened them.

For decision-making

UX value over personal taste.

Motion proposals could be evaluated against a written rubric instead of preference. Reviewers got faster; designers got clearer feedback; reasonable disagreements resolved on first principles.

For engineering

Alignment on tradeoffs.

Performance and implementation cost stopped being a post-design negotiation. They entered the conversation at briefing, with shared expectations and a known toolchain.

For the system

A foundation for CMS motion.

The guidelines mapped onto real CMS components, giving the design system a basis for consistent motion across marketing pages — without bespoke engineering per launch.

For the brand

Motion as strategy.

Animation got framed inside the team as a storytelling and usability tool — not a finishing layer or competitive reflex. The conversation matured.

For research

Permission to learn.

The animated-vs-static comparison normalized the idea that motion has to earn its place. Future motion work has a built-in habit of measuring, not just shipping.

"It stopped being a debate about whether the animation looked cool. It became a debate about whether the animation was doing anything. That's a different team." — Working note, motion strategy review
11 — What I Learned

Balancing delight and performance.

Four notes — written as advice for the next motion strategy I touch.

  1. L · 01

    Motion strategy is mostly a writing exercise.

    The artifact people actually used wasn't a Figma file — it was the document that named the categories, the principles, and the questions. Words give a team something to argue with; pretty prototypes only give them something to admire.

    Keep
  2. L · 02

    Refuse the "one tool to rule them all" instinct.

    Standardizing on a single tool would have been simpler to communicate and worse in practice. Letting Lottie and GSAP each own their best job — and writing the rules that pick between them — held up under pressure.

    Keep
  3. L · 03

    Treat performance and accessibility as design inputs.

    If those constraints enter the conversation only at handoff, the design is already wrong. Putting them in the brief — and in the decision framework — made motion proposals stronger, not smaller.

    Keep
  4. L · 04

    "Better than the static version" is the right bar.

    The most useful question I asked the team wasn't "is this animation good?" It was "is this animation doing something a thoughtful static version couldn't?" Most of the time, the static version got better as a result. That was the point.

    Keep
"The goal was never more animation. The goal was clearer storytelling, calmer feedback, and motion the team could defend on its merits — even when it meant choosing not to animate at all." — Closing reflection
