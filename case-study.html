01 — Overview

One system, not a hundred pages.

A long-running effort to retire SurveyMonkey's fragmented marketing site stack and replace it with one composable web system used by everyone who touches the site.

Blueprint isn't a redesign. The brand was healthy and the traffic was strong; the problem sat underneath the design layer, in the page model, the CMS, and the way three different teams were each authoring their own version of "the homepage." This case study walks through the strategy, the decisions worth flagging, and what changed once the system started landing in production.

02 — Context

The site had grown into three sites.

The marketing surface served roughly four audiences (researchers, CX teams, employee-experience buyers, and SMBs trying surveys for the first time) across four locales. Over a decade of growth, the implementation had accreted into three parallel CMSes, each with its own component vocabulary, motion habits, and update cadence.

Authors stitched pages from a long tail of one-off components. Engineers patched the live site to clear backlog. A brand refresh had landed in product but not on marketing — so the front door was visibly off from the rest of the experience.

Background

By the start of the project, the team supported 140-plus distinct page components and a similar number of one-off templates. The cost of a new page was no longer "design a page" — it was "decide which of three systems to use."

03 — Problem

The platform was holding the work hostage.

The visible symptom was slow page launches. The deeper problem was that no single team could change the marketing site safely on their own. Marketing waited on engineering; engineering waited on design; design waited on a brand decision that wasn't going to come.

What we kept hearing

  • "Every campaign feels like a new build."
  • "I don't know which components are blessed."
  • "We can't ship a localized variant without a ticket."
  • "Motion behaves differently per page."

What the data hinted at

  • Long page-launch lead times
  • High variance in performance budgets per page
  • Duplicate components doing similar jobs
  • Drift between marketing and product styling
04 — My Role

Lead designer & the person holding the spine.

I owned the design direction end-to-end: the page system, the component model, the motion vocabulary, the CMS schema work, and the cross-team alignment that the project lived or died by. On any given week I was switching between IC work, design crits, engineering API reviews, and exec readouts.

What I led
Page system · component library · tokens · motion · CMS schema · governance model
What I shipped personally
Architecture diagrams, the first 8 components, brand-to-token mapping, the motion primitives spec
What I delegated
Detailed component design, doc site implementation, locale-specific pattern variants
What I didn't own
Brand visual identity, product UX, marketing copy
05 — Strategy

Treat the marketing site as a product.

If a product team needs a roadmap, a design system, instrumentation, and a release model — so does this.

The strategy had three commitments. First: one system, not a refresh of three. We would invest where it compounded — the page model, the component library, the CMS schema — and let the visual brand follow tokens automatically. Second: authors are users. The CMS authoring experience was treated as a UX surface in its own right, with its own design crits. Third: ship as we go. We picked a real campaign every quarter to be a forcing function, instead of building in private.

Strategic bet

A small, opinionated set of composable templates would do more for velocity than a sprawling component library that "could build anything."

06 — Process

Six months of listening, then build in public.

Figure 01 — Process timeline
Fig 01A high-level view of the project arc — discovery, system foundations, pilot campaign, scale.

The first two months were almost entirely listening. We mapped every component in production, interviewed the marketers and engineers using them, and ran a quiet audit of the authoring experience by sitting next to authors as they shipped real pages.

From there we worked outward in concentric rings: tokens first, then primitives, then a small set of composable templates, then the CMS schema that exposed them to authors. We didn't try to migrate the whole site — instead, each quarter we picked one real campaign as the test of whether the system was ready.

07 — Key Decisions

The handful of choices that shaped everything after.

  1. D · 01

    Page templates over freeform pages

    A curated set of templates, each with a clear job, instead of a blank canvas. Less expressive — more reliable, more measurable, and far faster to ship in.

    Kept
  2. D · 02

    Tokens as the real brand surface

    Marketing visual changes route through tokens, not component-level styling. The site can be re-themed without re-shipping a single component.

    Kept
  3. D · 03

    CMS schema designed with authors

    We co-designed the authoring schema with marketers in the room, then made it the API for engineering. Authoring is a design surface, not an afterthought.

    Kept
  4. D · 04

    A motion vocabulary, not a motion library

    We picked a small set of motion primitives with timing tokens and a reduced-motion budget. Designers compose; they don't reach for the Lottie folder.

    Kept
  5. D · 05

    "Big bang" rewrite

    Initial plan called for a full migration in one cycle. We revised to a per-campaign rollout — slower, but with measurable wins each quarter and a clear off-ramp if priorities shifted.

    Revised
08 — System & UX Solution

A stack, not a Figma file.

Five layers, each owned by a clear team, each composable upward into a real marketing page.

Figure 03 — System architecture diagram
Fig 03From tokens to templates to authored pages — how a marketer's page request resolves into shipped pixels.

At the base sit tokens: color, type, motion timing, spacing, radii. Above that, primitives — buttons, headings, layouts, media — that consume tokens and nothing else. Then a curated set of composable components with clear jobs (Hero, FeatureRow, PricingTable, CTABlock). Above that, page templates: opinionated arrangements of components with sensible defaults. The top layer is the CMS schema, which exposes only the surface area authors should be making decisions about.

Principle

If a marketer can't ship a page without engineering, the system has failed. Boring at the bottom; fast at the top.

09 — Collaboration

Designed alongside marketing, engineering, and brand.

Blueprint had three sponsoring orgs and no single boss. That made cross-functional collaboration the actual work, not a side effect of it.

How we worked

  • Weekly system reviews open to anyone
  • A monthly "authoring lab" with PMMs
  • Pull-request-shaped design proposals
  • Shared OKR with engineering & PMM
  • Public roadmap in a shared Linear project

How we made decisions

  • Written one-page proposals before any meeting
  • Reversible decisions: ship and review in 4 weeks
  • One-way doors: stop, write a memo, defend it
  • Brand vetoes documented and dated
  • Authoring veto power for repeated UX pain
"The system felt designed with us instead of handed to us. That was the difference." — PMM, EMEA · partner team
10 — Impact

What changed downstream.

Impact is still landing — the project is in flight. The shifts below are qualitative observations from teams using the system, recorded in the first two pilot campaigns.

Velocity

Page launches feel routine.

Authors report that shipping a campaign page now resembles writing a brief instead of running a build. Engineering review remains, but as a check, not a bottleneck.

Consistency

The site looks like one site.

Brand has stopped flagging cross-page drift in weekly QA. Locale variants share a visual rhythm without explicit coordination.

Trust

Marketing & engineering relaxed.

The two teams that historically blocked each other are running off a shared backlog. Escalations to design dropped sharply.

Quantitative results are tracked per pilot campaign and shared internally as each one closes. I'm comfortable speaking to those numbers in a live conversation; I won't quote them here while the program is mid-flight.

11 — What I Learned

Three things I'd do the same, two I'd change.

  1. L · 01

    Make the authoring experience your hero.

    The component design was the easy half. The 10× gain came from designing the CMS form a marketer fills in — that's where velocity lives.

    Keep
  2. L · 02

    Use real campaigns as forcing functions.

    The temptation to build the system "fully" before letting anyone touch it is real and wrong. Every quarter we shipped, the system got better — because reality has a way of finding the gaps.

    Keep
  3. L · 03

    Governance is a design problem.

    Who can add a component? Who can override a token? Documented and answered up front, those questions save quarters of friction later.

    Keep
  4. L · 04

    I waited too long on motion.

    We treated motion as a phase-two concern. By the time the primitives landed, teams had already invented their own — which we then had to deprecate. Should have shipped a minimal motion vocabulary in week one.

    Change
  5. L · 05

    Under-invested in docs.

    A working component without a working doc is a half-shipped component. Next time, doc writing sits inside the definition-of-done, not after it.

    Change
